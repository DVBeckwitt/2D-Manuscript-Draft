The ordered Bi$_2$Se$_3$ and Bi$_2$Te$_3$ films provide the main experimental validation of the model. Together they test the central claims introduced earlier in the manuscript: the samples are well described by the 2D-powder orientational limit, the full area-detector image contains essential constraints that would be weakened by a conventional 1D reduction, and a detector-space forward model containing geometry, finite beam phase space, mosaicity, detector resolution, and ordered structure-factor weighting can reproduce measured line shapes. Both materials form layered, strongly oriented films whose detector patterns contain the two signatures expected for a 2D powder: off-specular Bragg arcs from in-plane HK families and specular-family intensity from $HK=0$ reflections. The validation test is therefore not whether a simulated image resembles the measurement qualitatively, but whether the same forward-model form, with material-specific structural parameters, reproduces peak position, width, asymmetry, tails, and relative intensity after the measured and calculated images are projected in the same way.

For each material, the comparison is shown in two complementary views. The detector-space panel identifies the fixed-$Q_r$/HK Bragg-rod trajectories used for projection while keeping the measured area-detector intensity visible. The profile panel then gives the quantitative comparison: measured and calculated intensity plotted on the same axes as a function of $Q_z$ for the selected HK families. The detector image is the map of where the profiles are extracted, and the $Q_z$ overlays are the primary evidence for the fit quality. This form of comparison preserves the information carried by the two-dimensional pattern because the extraction follows indexed detector-space trajectories rather than replacing the image with a single azimuthally averaged radial profile.

Reflection labels use representative HK families. Symmetry-equivalent in-plane reflections with the same $Q_r$ are grouped under one HK label in the figure, but that grouping is only a plotting convention. In the calculation, the individual symmetry-related reciprocal-lattice vectors are generated and projected separately before their detector-space contributions are combined. This distinction is important because equivalent HK reflections do not contribute with a single geometry-independent powder multiplicity in a 2D-powder grazing-incidence measurement. The plus and minus branches, where shown in profile panels, refer only to the two detector-side intersections of the same in-plane rod family and are not separate crystallographic families.

A central result of the ordered-film comparison is the need for a Lorentzian contribution to the mosaic distribution. The clearest evidence is the $HK=0$ specular family, i.e. the $00L$ peaks. For a perfectly aligned crystallite at the nominal incidence angle $\theta_i$, only the specular condition directly selected by that incidence geometry should carry appreciable intensity. The measured detector images and projected profiles instead contain additional $HK=0$ peaks whose Bragg conditions are not directly excited by $\theta_i$. A narrow Gaussian-like mosaic core can account for the strong near-condition arc widths, but it decays too rapidly to supply enough diffracting volume for these off-condition specular peaks. The Lorentzian tail represents the small population of crystallites tilted far enough from the mean film normal to bring those reflections onto the Ewald sphere. It is therefore a physical part of the mosaicity model rather than a numerical background correction.

A second important feature appears near the $HK=0$, $L=1$ reflection, where the measured image shows a star-like pattern together with bright intensity extending above the critical-angle region. This feature is not assigned to reflectivity. Instead, it is explained within the same diffraction picture by the finite bandwidth of the incident beam, which gives the Ewald sphere an effective thickness. Because the $HK=0$, $L=1$ Bragg sphere has comparatively small reciprocal-space radius, this finite-thickness Ewald shell illuminates a relatively large fraction of it. The result is enhanced intensity and the characteristic star-like morphology. This feature therefore provides an additional test that the forward model must treat the experimental phase space, not only the nominal central wavelength and incidence condition.

Figure~\ref{fig:ordered_chalcogenide_validation} gathers the ordered-film validation into a single matched comparison. Panels~(a) and~(b) are the detector-space views for Bi$_2$Se$_3$ and Bi$_2$Te$_3$, respectively. These panels identify the fixed-$Q_r$/HK trajectories used for projection and show both off-specular and $HK=0$ features in the measured images. Panels~(c) and~(d) show the corresponding projected $Q_z$ profiles for Bi$_2$Se$_3$ and Bi$_2$Te$_3$. In each profile panel, the measured and calculated intensities have been subjected to the same projection and integration procedure, so the overlays directly test the complete experimental model rather than an idealized reciprocal-space cut. Figure~\ref{fig:hk0_l1_star} then isolates the star-like $HK=0$, $L=1$ feature to emphasize that this intensity above the critical-angle region is explained by the finite Ewald-shell thickness associated with the incident bandwidth rather than by a reflectivity process.

The horizontal coordinate in the profile panels is reported as $Q_z$ along the selected trajectory. Because mosaicity, beam divergence, wavelength spread, incidence-angle uncertainty, detector resolution, and finite integration width all broaden the effective scattering condition, the projected coordinate should not be interpreted as an ideal reciprocal-space cut from a single perfect crystallite. The comparison remains meaningful because the calculated image is subjected to the same projection and resolution effects before it is compared with the measured profile. The figure therefore tests the detector-space calculation as used experimentally, not a simplified line cut through an unbroadened reciprocal lattice.

In Fig.~\ref{fig:ordered_chalcogenide_validation}, the off-specular HK profiles and the $HK=0$ profiles play complementary roles. The off-specular arcs establish that the 2D-powder reciprocal-space construction and calibrated detector geometry put the selected Bragg-rod trajectories in the correct places. Their widths and asymmetries constrain the Gaussian-like mosaic core together with instrumental resolution. The $HK=0$ peaks that are not directly excited by the nominal $\theta_i$ condition then test the Lorentzian tail specifically. A model with only a rapidly decaying mosaic core could fit the main arcs while missing this weak specular-family intensity, so the $HK=0$ rows provide the visible evidence that the long-tail mosaic population is required.

Figure~\ref{fig:hk0_l1_star} highlights a second feature that depends on the experimental phase-space treatment. The bright star-like intensity near $HK=0$, $L=1$ extends above the critical-angle region, but is not a reflectivity signal. Instead, it arises because the finite bandwidth gives the Ewald sphere an effective thickness, allowing a large portion of the small-radius $HK=0$, $L=1$ Bragg sphere to contribute simultaneously. This makes the morphology near $HK=0$, $L=1$ an additional check that the forward model must include wavelength bandwidth explicitly.

This combined figure also closes the staged refinement workflow. Detector-space peak positions test the instrumental geometry, incidence condition, sample offsets, and goniometer misalignment. Local widths and tails test the two-component mosaic distribution and resolution terms. The off-condition $HK=0$ specular peaks test the Lorentzian mosaic contribution. Relative Bragg intensities test the ordered structure-factor description.
