Ordered Bi$_2$Se$_3$ and Bi$_2$Te$_3$ films validate the model by showing that the same detector-space forward model quantitatively reproduces their measured line shapes and intensities. In the fitting process, three detector images with incident angles of  $5^\circ$, $10^\circ$, and $15^\circ$ were used. Together, they test the 2D-powder orientational limit, with sample geometry, beam phase space, mosaicity, detector resolution, and ordered structure-factor weighting. Both materials exhibit the expected 2D-powder signatures: off-specular Bragg arcs from in-plane $m$ families and specular-family intensity from $m=0$ reflections. Here $m$ is the scalar in-plane family label defined in Sec.~\ref{sec:structure_factor}.

For each material, the comparison is shown in two complementary views. The detector-space panel identifies the fixed-$m$ Bragg-rod trajectories used for projection while keeping the measured area-detector intensity visible. The profile panel compares measured and calculated intensity plotted on the same axes as a function of $L$ for the selected $m$ families. Those rods are generated and projected separately before their detector-space contributions are summed. The plus and minus branches, where shown in profile panels, refer only to the two detector-side intersections of the same in-plane rod family and are not separate crystallographic families.

The ordered-film comparison also shows why the Lorentzian mosaic tail is needed. The clearest evidence is the $m=0$ specular family, i.e. the $00L$ peaks. For a perfectly aligned crystallite at the nominal incidence angle $\theta_i$, only the specular condition directly selected by that incidence geometry should carry appreciable intensity. The measured detector images and projected profiles instead contain additional $m=0$ peaks whose Bragg conditions are not directly excited by $\theta_i$. A narrow Gaussian-like mosaic core reproduces the strong near-condition arc widths, but it decays too rapidly to supply enough diffracting volume for these off-condition specular peaks.

The $003$ condition, or $m=0$, $L=3$, is the most visible example (Fig.~\ref{fig:m0_l3_star}). The measured image shows a star-like feature and bright intensity above the critical-angle region. We do not assign this intensity to reflectivity or to substrate effects. In the forward model, finite incident bandwidth gives the Ewald sphere an effective thickness, and the Lorentzian tail supplies crystallites tilted far enough from the mean orientation to bring the $003$ Bragg sphere into the accepted scattering condition.

Because mosaicity, beam divergence, wavelength spread, incidence-angle uncertainty, detector-pixel size, and finite integration width all broaden the effective scattering condition, the projected coordinate should not be interpreted as an ideal reciprocal-space cut from a single perfect crystallite. The detector-pixel contribution to the transformed-coordinate uncertainty is discussed in Sec.~\ref{sec:discussion_conclusion}: the radial angular term remains bounded, while the azimuthal term becomes important mainly near the low-$2\theta$ specular limit. The ordered-film profiles are therefore interpreted through the projection itself, not through an assumption that each plotted point is an exact point in reciprocal space. The comparison remains meaningful because the calculated image is subjected to the same projection, binning, and resolution effects before it is compared with the measured profile.

In Fig.~\ref{fig:ordered_chalcogenide_validation}, the off-specular $m$ and $m=0$ profiles play complementary roles. The off-specular arcs check the 2D-powder reciprocal-space construction and calibrated detector geometry, while their widths and asymmetries constrain the Gaussian-like mosaic core and instrumental resolution. The off-condition $m=0$ peaks test the Lorentzian tail and show why a rapidly decaying mosaic core alone is insufficient. The bandwidth-sensitive $003$ morphology isolated in Fig.~\ref{fig:m0_l3_star} reinforces that the forward model must include finite wavelength bandwidth explicitly.
