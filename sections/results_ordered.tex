Ordered Bi$_2$Se$_3$ and Bi$_2$Te$_3$ films validate the model by showing that the same detector-space forward model quantitatively reproduces measured line shapes and intensities in two related layered chalcogenides. Three detector images, collected at incident angles of $5^\circ$, $10^\circ$, and $15^\circ$, were used in the refinement. Figure~\ref{fig:ordered_chalcogenide_validation} shows the $5^\circ$ images and the associated projected profiles because this incidence condition gives the clearest single-view display of both the off-specular arc families and the specular $HK=0$ features. Together, the data test the 2D-powder orientational limit, sample geometry, finite beam phase space, mosaicity, detector resolution, and ordered structure-factor weighting.

The figure is meant to be read as both a validation plot and a result figure. The off-specular $HK=1$, 3, and 4 profiles show that the calculated 2D-powder geometry places the arc intersections at the correct detector positions and reproduces their $L$-dependent line shapes. More unexpectedly, the same images contain strong specular-family intensity from $HK=0$ reflections that are not all selected by the nominal incidence condition. In a narrowly aligned film with only the central incidence angle and a rapidly decaying mosaic distribution, only a small subset of the $00L$ family would carry appreciable intensity. The measured $HK=0$ profiles instead show additional $00L$ peaks, including weak off-condition peaks that remain visible on a semilogarithmic scale. These peaks provide the most direct evidence that the mosaic distribution has a long tail rather than only a narrow Gaussian-like core.

A second nontrivial observation is the bright low-$L$ intensity near the base of the $HK=0$ trajectory in the detector images. This intensity lies close to the direct-beam, beamstop, and critical-angle region and is not expected from an ideal narrow-band Bragg selection of isolated $00L$ peaks. It is therefore not treated as ordinary reflectivity in the ordered-film comparison. Instead, it is used as a qualitative check on whether the forward model includes the relevant experimental phase space: finite wavelength bandwidth, finite angular divergence, sample mosaicity, and the effective thickness of the Ewald shell. Because the low-$L$ Bragg sphere is small in reciprocal-space radius, those finite-width effects illuminate a comparatively large angular fraction of the near-origin specular region and can produce the observed star-like detector morphology.

The detector-space panels and profile panels in Fig.~\ref{fig:ordered_chalcogenide_validation} use the same extraction convention. In panels~(a) and~(b), the solid colored curves are the calculated centerlines of selected fixed-$Q_r$/HK Bragg-rod trajectories on the detector. The semi-transparent colored bands are the finite detector regions integrated to form the one-dimensional profiles. The dashed colored outlines mark the edges of those integration bands; they are not additional reflections. The central blue band is the $HK=0$ specular-family trajectory, while the paired off-specular bands are the two detector-side intersections of the same 2D-powder rod family. The plus and minus labels identify those two branches only; they do not denote different crystallographic families. Each $HK$ label denotes a family of symmetry-equivalent rods with the same $Q_r$, which are generated and projected separately before their detector-space contributions are summed.

Panels~(c) and~(d) show the corresponding line profiles after applying the same projection and integration operation to the measured and calculated images. The horizontal coordinate is $L=Q_z/|\bm c^*|$, so a marker labeled $L=n$ denotes the nominal $00n$ or $HKn$ Bragg condition for that selected rod family. The black solid curves are the measured profiles. The orange dashed curves are the simulated profiles after the same detector-space projection. Open circles mark the indexed Bragg/fit positions used to orient the comparison. The off-specular rows are plotted on a linear intensity scale because they test peak positions, relative intensities, and arc widths. The $HK=0$ row is plotted on a logarithmic scale because the important result is the persistence of weak, unexpected $00L$ intensity far from the strongest specular peak.

Because mosaicity, beam divergence, wavelength spread, incidence-angle uncertainty, detector resolution, and finite integration width all broaden the effective scattering condition, the projected coordinate should not be interpreted as an ideal reciprocal-space cut from a single perfect crystallite. The comparison remains meaningful because the calculated image is subjected to the same projection and resolution effects before it is compared with the measured profile. The figure therefore tests the detector-space calculation as used experimentally.

In Fig.~\ref{fig:ordered_chalcogenide_validation}, the off-specular and $HK=0$ profiles play complementary roles. The off-specular arcs check the 2D-powder reciprocal-space construction and calibrated detector geometry, while their widths and asymmetries constrain the Gaussian-like mosaic core and instrumental resolution. The off-condition $HK=0$ peaks test the Lorentzian mosaic tail and show why a rapidly decaying mosaic core alone is insufficient. Figure~\ref{fig:hk0_low_l_star} isolates the low-$L$ specular-region feature, emphasizing that the ordered chalcogenide images contain more information than peak positions alone: the unexpected $00L$ peaks and near-origin specular intensity both require the calculation to include the finite beam phase space and the tails of the out-of-plane orientation distribution.
