Ordered Bi$_2$Se$_3$ and Bi$_2$Te$_3$ films validate the model by showing that the same detector-space forward model quantitatively reproduces their measured line shapes and intensities. Together, they test the 2D-powder orientational limit, with sample geometry, beam phase space, mosaicity, detector resolution, and ordered structure-factor weighting. Both materials exhibit the expected 2D-powder signatures: off-specular Bragg arcs from in-plane $HK$ families and specular-family intensity from $HK=0$ reflections.

For each material, the comparison is shown in two complementary views. The detector-space panel identifies the fixed-$HK$ Bragg-rod trajectories used for projection while keeping the measured area-detector intensity visible. The profile panel compares measured and calculated intensity plotted on the same axes as a function of $L$ for the selected $HK$ families. The detector image is the map of where the profiles are extracted. Each $HK$ label denotes a family of symmetry-equivalent rods with the same $Q_r$. Those rods are generated and projected separately before their detector-space contributions are summed. The plus and minus branches, where shown in profile panels, refer only to the two detector-side intersections of the same in-plane rod family and are not separate crystallographic families.

A central result of the ordered-film comparison is the need for a Lorentzian contribution to the mosaic distribution. The clearest evidence is the $HK=0$ specular family, i.e. the $00L$ peaks. For a perfectly aligned crystallite at the nominal incidence angle $\theta_i$, only the specular condition directly selected by that incidence geometry should carry appreciable intensity. The measured detector images and projected profiles instead contain additional $HK=0$ peaks whose Bragg conditions are not directly excited by $\theta_i$. A narrow Gaussian-like mosaic core can account for the strong near-condition arc widths, but it decays too rapidly to supply enough diffracting volume for these off-condition specular peaks. The Lorentzian tail represents crystallites tilted far enough from the mean film normal to bring those reflections onto the Ewald sphere.

A second important feature appears near the $HK=0$, $L=1$ reflection, where the measured image shows a star-like pattern together with bright intensity extending above the critical-angle region. This feature is not assigned to reflectivity. Instead, it is explained within the same diffraction picture by the finite bandwidth of the incident beam, which gives the Ewald sphere an effective thickness. Because the $HK=0$, $L=1$ Bragg sphere has comparatively small reciprocal-space radius, this finite-thickness Ewald shell illuminates a relatively large fraction of it. The result is enhanced intensity and the characteristic star-like morphology. This feature therefore provides an additional test that the forward model must treat the experimental phase space, not only the nominal central wavelength and incidence condition.

Figure~\ref{fig:ordered_chalcogenide_validation} gathers the ordered-film validation into a single matched comparison. Panels~(a) and~(b) are the detector-space views for Bi$_2$Se$_3$ and Bi$_2$Te$_3$, respectively. These panels identify the fixed-$Q_r$/HK trajectories used for projection and show both off-specular and $HK=0$ features in the measured images. Panels~(c) and~(d) show the corresponding projected $Q_z$ profiles for Bi$_2$Se$_3$ and Bi$_2$Te$_3$. In each profile panel, the measured and calculated intensities have been subjected to the same projection and integration procedure.

Because mosaicity, beam divergence, wavelength spread, incidence-angle uncertainty, detector resolution, and finite integration width all broaden the effective scattering condition, the projected coordinate should not be interpreted as an ideal reciprocal-space cut from a single perfect crystallite. The comparison remains meaningful because the calculated image is subjected to the same projection and resolution effects before it is compared with the measured profile. The figure therefore tests the detector-space calculation as used experimentally.

In Fig.~\ref{fig:ordered_chalcogenide_validation}, the off-specular $HK$ and $HK=0$ profiles play complementary roles. The off-specular arcs check the 2D-powder reciprocal-space construction and calibrated detector geometry, while their widths and asymmetries constrain the Gaussian-like mosaic core and instrumental resolution. The off-condition $HK=0$ peaks test the Lorentzian tail and show why a rapidly decaying mosaic core alone is insufficient. Figure~\ref{fig:hk0_l1_star} isolates the bandwidth-sensitive $HK=0$, $L=1$ morphology, reinforcing that the forward model must include finite wavelength bandwidth explicitly.
