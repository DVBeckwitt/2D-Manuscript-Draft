\section{Diffraction Model}
\label{sec:diffraction_model}

We model the measured area-detector intensity by combining a calibrated instrument geometry with a crystallographic scattering model and a low-dimensional mosaicity spread appropriate to two-dimensional powders. The forward calculation enumerates allowed reciprocal-lattice vectors $\mathbf{G}$, samples rays from the finite-divergence incident beam, applies the diffraction condition, and propagates diffracted rays to the detector so that simulated images can be compared to experiment on a pixel-by-pixel basis. The essential elements are introduced below: (i) the beam, sample, and detector geometry that defines the pixel-to-$\mathbf{Q}$ mapping; (ii) the crystallographic structure factors that set reflection intensities; and (iii) the mosaicity model that redistributes intensity about each ideal Bragg condition.

\subsection{Diffraction Geometry}

In the lab frame, the incident wavevector $\mathbf{k}_i$ propagates along the $y$ axis, with a product of two Gaussian profiles in the $x$ and $z$ directions, as shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:waxs_geometry_system}. We treat the incident beam as a distribution in transverse position (widths $w_x,w_z$) and direction (rms divergences $\Delta\theta_x,\Delta\theta_z$). A Monte Carlo ray-tracing approach samples rays from this beam phase space and propagates each ray to the sample and then to the detector; simulated detector images are formed by accumulation over these rays.

The goniometer defines a rotation axis about which the sample normal rotates, as shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:waxs_geometry_sample_triptych}. In the aligned configuration, a rectangular film of in-plane dimensions $W$ and $H$ lies in the $x$-$y$ plane with thickness $t$ along the sample normal. The rotation axis lies within this plane, with the incident beam parallel to the $x$-$y$ plane and perpendicular to the rotation axis. In practice, the beam center and sample center may be displaced from the goniometer axis by vertical offsets $z_B$ and $z_S$, respectively. Rotating the sample about this axis sets the incidence angle $\theta_i$ between the incident wavevector $\mathbf{k}_i$ and the sample surface, as shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:waxs_geometry_system}. If the substrate is not perfectly parallel to the $x$-$y$ plane (e.g., it is not flush against the sample holder), the sample normal can also be tilted by a residual angle $\delta$. For each trial ray, we compute its intersection with the sample plane. The finite beam size at grazing incidence produces an elongated footprint of length $X_s$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:waxs_geometry_sample_triptych}); rays that fall outside the finite illuminated window set by $W$ and $H$ miss the sample and are discarded. Because rays are sampled from the spatial beam profile, partial illumination and footprint truncation are naturally captured.

For rays that intersect the film, wave attenuation occurs both at the surface (Fresnel transmission) and during propagation through the medium (absorption). We model the film by a complex refractive index $\tilde{n}=1-\delta_n+i\beta_n$, compute transmitted internal wavevectors using Snell's law together with continuity of the in-plane components, and use the resulting complex out-of-plane components to set the attenuation along the incident and exit paths through thickness $t$. Full expressions for the internal wavevectors, Fresnel factors, and thickness-averaged absorption weight are given in the supporting information (Sec.~S1.2).

The 2D area detector is positioned at a distance $D$ from the sample along the lab $y$ axis, with tilts about the $x$ and $z$ axes denoted $\beta$ and $\gamma$, respectively, as shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:waxs_geometry_system}. An additional in-plane detector rotation $\chi$ accounts for rotation of the detector axes within the detector plane. A diffracted ray with exit wavevector $\mathbf{k}_f$ in the lab frame intersects the detector at a scattering angle $2\theta$, defined as the angle between $\mathbf{k}_f$ and the $y$ axis, and at a detector-plane azimuth $\phi$, measured from the detector $x'$ axis.

\subsection{Structure Factor}

The crystallographic model supplies both the set of reflections that can contribute to the image and the intrinsic weight of each reflection. We enumerate all symmetry-allowed Miller triplets $(h,k,l)$ whose scattering angle $2\theta$ falls within the detector acceptance, and for each one construct the reciprocal-lattice vector $\mathbf G$. Conventional powder calculations often replace symmetry-equivalent reflections by a multiplicity factor. We do not do that here. In a two-dimensional powder, the measured intensity of symmetry-related reflections depends on the experimental geometry and on the crystallite orientation distribution, so the effective multiplicity is not a single geometry-independent number. We therefore retain every allowed $\mathbf G$ explicitly, compute the corresponding exit wavevector $\mathbf{k}_f$, and let multiplicity and orientation effects emerge within the forward model. The crystal structure enters through an intrinsic per-reflection weight $I_{0,\mathbf G}\propto \lvert F_{\mathbf G}\rvert^2$, while beam-geometry, absorption, and mosaicity effects are applied separately.

For each reflection with reciprocal-lattice vector
\[
\mathbf G = h\mathbf a^{*} + k\mathbf b^{*} + l\mathbf c^{*},
\]
we compute the structure factor as
\begin{equation}
F_{\mathbf G} =
\sum_{a}
o_a\, f_a(Q)\,
\exp\!\left[2\pi i\, \mathbf G \cdot \mathbf r_a\right]\,
\exp\!\left[-2\pi^{2}\,\mathbf G\cdot\mathbf U_a\cdot\mathbf G\right],
\label{eq:structure_factor}
\end{equation}
where the sum runs over atoms $a$ in the unit cell, $o_a$ is the site occupancy, $f_a(Q)$ is the
atomic form factor evaluated at the momentum transfer of that reflection, and
$\mathbf r_a=(x_a,y_a,z_a)$ gives the fractional atomic position generated by the space-group
symmetry (Wyckoff site), so that $\mathbf G\cdot\mathbf r_a \equiv hx_a+ky_a+lz_a$. If we refine
positions, we vary only the free parameters of the occupied Wyckoff sites, not independent
per-atom shifts. The Debye--Waller factor is written in anisotropic tensor form, where
$\mathbf U_a$ is the mean-square displacement tensor (ADP) for atom $a$, expressed in the same
basis convention used for $\mathbf G$. In refinement, $\mathbf U_a$ may be constrained by symmetry
or reduced parameterizations when warranted by the data.
