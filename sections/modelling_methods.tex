\section{Modelling Methods}

\subsection{Structure Factor}

Quantitative refinement of two-dimensional X-ray diffraction images requires a crystal structure model, which determines the structure factors $F_{\mathbf{G}}$ and the associated set of reciprocal-lattice vectors $\mathbf{G}$. For a given reflection, the magnitude $\lvert F_{\mathbf{G}}\rvert$ governs the scattered intensity, while $\mathbf{G}$ sets the diffraction condition through the intersection of the Ewald sphere with reciprocal space. We therefore enumerate all $\mathbf{G}$ whose scattering angle $2\theta$ falls within the detector acceptance. Standard approaches reduce cost by grouping symmetry-equivalent reflections via a multiplicity factor and scaling the intensity accordingly. For two-dimensional powders, where the experimental geometry and texture can make multiplicity nontrivial and structure-dependent, we instead evaluate every allowed $\mathbf{G}$ explicitly. For each $\mathbf{G}$ we compute $\lvert F_{\mathbf{G}}\rvert^{2}$ together with the corresponding exit wavevector $\mathbf{k}_f$, so that multiplicity and texture emerge naturally within the forward model.

For each reflection with reciprocal-lattice vector
\[
\mathbf{G}=h\mathbf{a}^{*}+k\mathbf{b}^{*}+l\mathbf{c}^{*},
\]
we compute the structure factor as
\begin{equation}
  F_{\mathbf{G}}
  =
  \sum_{a} o_{a}\,f_{a}(Q)\,
  \exp\!\Bigl[ 2\pi i\,\mathbf{G}\cdot\bigl(\mathbf{r}_{a}+\Delta\mathbf{r}_{a}\bigr) \Bigr]\,
  \exp\!\Bigl[ -2\pi^{2}\bigl(U_{r,a} G_{r}^{2}+U_{z,a} G_{z}^{2}\bigr) \Bigr],
  \label{eq:structure_factor}
\end{equation}
where the sum runs over atoms $a$ in the unit cell, $o_a$ is the site occupancy,
$f_a(Q)$ is the atomic form factor with $Q=\lvert\mathbf{G}\rvert$,
$\mathbf{r}_a$ is the ideal atomic position, and $\Delta\mathbf{r}_a$ is a refined static displacement.
The anisotropic Debye--Waller term uses mean-square displacements $U_{r,a}$ and $U_{z,a}$ for the in-plane and out-of-plane components of $\mathbf{G}$, denoted $G_r$ and $G_z$ and defined relative to the layer normal.


\subsection{Diffraction Geometry}

In the lab frame the incident wavevector $\mathbf{k}_i$ propagates along the $y$ axis with a product of two Gaussian profiles in the $x$ and $z$ directions, as sketched in Fig.~\ref{fig:waxs_geometry_system}. A Markov approach draws rays from this beam distribution with a similar Gaussian divergence and propagates each ray to the sample and subsequently to the detector.

The goniometer defines a rotation axis about which the sample normal rotates, shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:waxs_geometry_sample_full}. In the aligned configuration a rectangular film of in plane width $W$ and height $H$ lies in the $x$-$y$ plane with thickness $t$ along the sample normal. The rotation axis lies within this plane and the incident beam is parallel to the $x$-$y$ plane and perpendicular to the rotation axis. In practice the beam and sample can be displaced from the goniometer axis by distances $z_b$ and $z_s$, respectively. Rotating the sample about this axis then sets the incidence angle $\theta_i$ between the incident wavevector $\mathbf{k}_i$ and the sample surface, as indicated in Fig.~\ref{fig:waxs_geometry_system}. If the substrate is not perfectly parallel to the $x$-$y$ plane (for example, it does not sit flush against the sample holder) the sample normal can also be tilted by an additional angle $\chi$. For each trial ray we compute its intersection with the sample plane. Rays that intersect outside the finite footprint set by $W$ and $H$ miss the sample and are discarded. For rays that hit the film the thickness $t$ sets the path length through the material and the corresponding absorption weighting.

The 2D area detector is positioned a distance $D$ from the sample along the lab $y$ axis, with tilts about the $x$ and $z$ axes denoted $\beta$ and $\gamma$, respectively, as shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:waxs_geometry_system}. A diffracted ray with exit wavevector $\mathbf{k}_f$ in the lab frame intersects the detector at a scattering angle $2\theta$, defined as the angle between $\mathbf{k}_f$ and the $y$ axis, and at an azimuthal angle $\phi$ measured in the detector plane from the in plane $y'$ axis that defines the detector frame.
