\section{Diffraction Model}

\subsection{Model overview}
We compute full area-detector images by forward modelling the measured WAXS geometry.
For each trial parameter set, we (i) enumerate the allowed reciprocal-lattice vectors $\mathbf{G}$ within the detector acceptance, (ii) sample incident rays from a finite beam with widths $(w_x,w_z)$ and angular divergences $(\Delta\theta_x,\Delta\theta_z)$, (iii) propagate each ray through the sample alignment and footprint to obtain the exit wavevector $\mathbf{k}_f$, (iv) map $\mathbf{k}_f$ to detector coordinates using the calibrated detector geometry, and (v) sum contributions to predict per-pixel intensities.
Geometry parameters control the pixel-to-$\mathbf{Q}$ mapping, while mosaicity and structure factors control line shapes and reflection-to-reflection intensity ratios.

\subsection{Diffraction geometry}
In the lab frame, the incident wavevector $\mathbf{k}_i$ propagates along $\hat{y}$ with a separable Gaussian profile in $x$ and $z$ (widths $w_x,w_z$) and Gaussian angular divergence about $\hat{y}$ (standard deviations $\Delta\theta_x,\Delta\theta_z$), as shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:waxs_geometry_system}.
A Monte Carlo approach samples rays from this distribution, propagates them to the sample, and then propagates diffracted rays to the detector.

The goniometer defines a rotation axis about which the sample normal rotates (Fig.~\ref{fig:waxs_geometry_sample_triptych}).
In the aligned configuration, a rectangular film of in-plane dimensions $W$ and $H$ lies in the $x$--$y$ plane with thickness $t$ along the sample normal.
In practice, the beam and sample may be displaced from the goniometer axis by distances $z_b$ and $z_s$, respectively.
Rotating the sample about this axis sets the incidence angle $\theta_i$ between $\mathbf{k}_i$ and the sample surface.
If the substrate is not perfectly parallel to the $x$--$y$ plane (e.g., not flush against the holder), the sample normal can be tilted by an additional small angle (shown as $\chi$ in Fig.~\ref{fig:waxs_geometry_sample_triptych}).

For each sampled incident ray, we compute its intersection with the sample plane.
Rays that fall outside the finite footprint set by $W$ and $H$ are discarded.
Rays that intersect the film are weighted by (i) footprint and thickness, and (ii) absorption along the ray path through the film.
When needed at grazing incidence, refraction can be incorporated by computing the transmitted wavevector inside the film from the complex refractive index and applying the corresponding transmission and attenuation factors to the ray weight.

The 2D area detector is positioned at distance $D$ along $\hat{y}$, with tilts about $\hat{x}$ and $\hat{z}$ denoted $\beta$ and $\gamma$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:waxs_geometry_system}).
A diffracted ray with exit wavevector $\mathbf{k}_f$ intersects the detector at scattering angle $2\theta$ (angle between $\mathbf{k}_f$ and $\hat{y}$) and azimuth $\phi$ measured in the detector plane from the detector $y'$ axis.

\subsection{Mosaicity}
Imperfect 2D powders exhibit out-of-plane mosaicity, with crystallite layer normals distributed about the substrate normal.
We parameterize this by a misorientation density $\omega(\Delta\theta)$, where $\Delta\theta$ is the polar tilt angle between a crystallite layer normal and the substrate normal.
We take $\omega$ to be a unit-normalized mixture of a Lorentzian and a Gaussian:
\begin{equation}
\omega(\Delta\theta) =
\eta\,\frac{1}{\pi}\frac{\gamma}{\Delta\theta^{2}+\gamma^{2}}
+ (1-\eta)\,\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma}\exp\!\left(-\frac{\Delta\theta^{2}}{2\sigma^{2}}\right),
\qquad 0 \le \eta \le 1,
\label{eq:mosaic_kernel}
\end{equation}
with Lorentzian half-width $\gamma$ and Gaussian standard deviation $\sigma$.
Because $\Delta\theta$ is defined modulo $2\pi$, we use the wrapped, $2\pi$-periodic form (see SI) to avoid overcounting.

\subsection{Structure factors}
Quantitative refinement requires a crystal structure model that determines structure factors $F_{\mathbf{G}}$ and the associated reciprocal-lattice vectors $\mathbf{G}$.
For each allowed $\mathbf{G}$ within the detector acceptance, we compute $\lvert F_{\mathbf{G}}\rvert^2$ together with the corresponding exit wavevector $\mathbf{k}_f$ so that multiplicity emerges from the explicit enumeration rather than an external correction.

For each reflection with reciprocal-lattice vector
\[
\mathbf{G}=h\mathbf{a}^{*}+k\mathbf{b}^{*}+l\mathbf{c}^{*},
\]
we compute
\begin{equation}
F_{\mathbf{G}} =
\sum_{a} o_a\, f_a(Q)\,
\exp\!\left(2\pi i\,\mathbf{G}\cdot\mathbf{r}_a\right)\,
\exp\!\left[-2\pi^{2}\,\mathbf{G}^{\mathsf{T}}\mathbf{U}_a\,\mathbf{G}\right],
\label{eq:structure_factor}
\end{equation}
where the sum runs over atoms $a$ in the unit cell, $o_a$ is the site occupancy, $f_a(Q)$ is the atomic form factor with $Q=\lvert\mathbf{G}\rvert$, $\mathbf{r}_a$ is the fractional atomic position fixed by the occupied Wyckoff site, and $\mathbf{U}_a$ is the anisotropic Debye--Waller displacement tensor.
In refinements where the data do not constrain a full tensor, we impose axial symmetry about the layer normal, $\mathbf{U}_a=\mathrm{diag}(U_{r,a},U_{r,a},U_{z,a})$ in a basis aligned with the layer normal, which reduces the exponent to $-2\pi^2(U_{r,a}G_r^2+U_{z,a}G_z^2)$ with $G_r^2=G_x^2+G_y^2$.
