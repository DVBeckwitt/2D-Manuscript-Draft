\section{Modelling Methods}

\subsection{Structure Factor}

Quantitative refinement of two-dimensional X-ray diffraction images requires a crystal structure model, which determines the structure factors $F_{\mathbf{G}}$ and the associated set of reciprocal-lattice vectors $\mathbf{G}$. For a given reflection, the magnitude $\lvert F_{\mathbf{G}}\rvert$ governs the scattered intensity, while $\mathbf{G}$ sets the diffraction condition through the intersection of the Ewald sphere with reciprocal space. We therefore enumerate all $\mathbf{G}$ whose scattering angle $2\theta$ falls within the detector acceptance. Standard approaches reduce cost by grouping symmetry-equivalent reflections via a multiplicity factor and scaling the intensity accordingly. For two-dimensional powders, where the experimental geometry and texture can make multiplicity nontrivial and structure-dependent, we instead evaluate every allowed $\mathbf{G}$ explicitly. For each $\mathbf{G}$ we compute $\lvert F_{\mathbf{G}}\rvert^{2}$ together with the corresponding exit wavevector $\mathbf{k}_f$, so that multiplicity and texture emerge naturally within the forward model.

For each reflection with reciprocal-lattice vector
\[
\mathbf G = h\mathbf a^{*} + k\mathbf b^{*} + l\mathbf c^{*},
\]
we compute the structure factor as
\begin{equation}
F_{\mathbf G} =
\sum_{a}
o_a\, f_a(Q)\,
\exp\!\left[2\pi i\, \mathbf G \cdot \mathbf r_a\right]\,
\exp\!\left[-2\pi^{2}\left(U_{r,a}G_r^{2}+U_{z,a}G_z^{2}\right)\right],
\tag{1}
\end{equation}
where the sum runs over atoms $a$ in the unit cell, $o_a$ is the site occupancy, $f_a(Q)$ is the
atomic form factor with $Q=|\mathbf G|$, and $\mathbf r_a$ is the fractional atomic position
set by the space-group symmetry (Wyckoff site). If we refine positions, we vary only the free
parameters of the occupied Wyckoff sites, not independent per-atom shifts. The Debye--Waller
factor uses mean-square displacements $U_{r,a}$ and $U_{z,a}$ for in-plane and out-of-plane
motion, applied to the corresponding components of $\mathbf G$ ($G_r$ and $G_z$) defined
relative to the layer normal. This reduced form is an approximation chosen because the data do
not reliably constrain a more detailed parameterization.

\subsection{Diffraction Geometry}

In the lab frame, the incident wavevector $\mathbf{k}_i$ propagates along the $y$ axis, with a product of two Gaussian profiles in the $x$ and $z$ directions, as shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:waxs_geometry_system}. A Markov approach samples rays from this beam distribution, accounting for Gaussian divergence, and propagates each ray to the sample and then to the detector.

The goniometer defines a rotation axis about which the sample normal rotates, as shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:waxs_geometry_sample_full}. In the aligned configuration, a rectangular film of in-plane dimensions $W$ and $H$ lies in the $x$-$y$ plane with thickness $t$ along the sample normal. The rotation axis lies within this plane, with the incident beam parallel to the $x$-$y$ plane and perpendicular to the rotation axis. In practice, the beam and sample may be displaced from the goniometer axis by distances $z_b$ and $z_s$, respectively. Rotating the sample about this axis sets the incidence angle $\theta_i$ between the incident wavevector $\mathbf{k}_i$ and the sample surface, as shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:waxs_geometry_system}. If the substrate is not perfectly parallel to the $x$-$y$ plane (e.g., it is not flush against the sample holder), the sample normal can also be tilted by an additional angle $\chi$. For each trial ray, we compute its intersection with the sample plane. Rays that fall outside the finite footprint set by $W$ and $H$ miss the sample and are discarded. Rays that intersect the film are weighted by their path length through the material, with absorption determined by thickness $t$.

The 2D area detector is positioned at a distance $D$ from the sample along the lab $y$ axis, with tilts about the $x$ and $z$ axes denoted $\beta$ and $\gamma$, respectively, as shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:waxs_geometry_system}. A diffracted ray with exit wavevector $\mathbf{k}_f$ in the lab frame intersects the detector at a scattering angle $2\theta$, defined as the angle between $\mathbf{k}_f$ and the $y$ axis, and at an azimuthal angle $\phi$, measured in the detector plane from the in-plane $y'$ axis that defines the detector frame.
