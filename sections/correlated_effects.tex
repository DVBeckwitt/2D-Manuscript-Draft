\subsection{Mosaicity, bandwidth, and Bragg-sphere size}
\label{sec:correlated_effects}

The observed intensities are a result of the convlolution of the bandwidth, mosaic, and incident angle. The Bragg sphere corresponds to a different crystallite orientation for the same reciprocal-lattice-vector magnitude. Mosaicity populates only a cap-like region of that Bragg sphere for m=0. The observed intensity is therefore the part of that allowed ring that overlaps the populated mosaic cap.

Finite wavelength bandwidth modifies the Ewald sphere into a shell of allowed radii. Beam divergence and incident-angle spread have a similar effect by moving the Ewald-sphere center and direction. When this Ewald shell intersects the mosaic-populated Bragg sphere, the overlap region's morphology is altered. This is prominent for low-order reflections as a small Bragg sphere with a fixed instrumental bandwidth covers comparatively large fraction of the Bragg sphere and can continue to overlap the mosaic cap over a broad range of nominal incident angles. For a larger Bragg sphere, the same bandwidth produces a narrower effective intersection, so the detected intensity is more sensitive to the exact incidence condition.

This geometry explains a counterintuitive feature of the measurements. Increasing the incidence angle can bring high-order $00L$ peaks into condition while the low-order peaks remain visible, but lowering the incidence angle does not necessarily preserve the high-order peaks. The low-order peaks are robust because the finite Ewald-shell thickness and the mosaic cap continue to overlap. The high-order peaks require a more specific intersection because their Bragg spheres are larger and the same bandwidth samples a smaller fraction of the populated cap.
