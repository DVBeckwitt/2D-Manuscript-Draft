\subsection{Mosaicity}
\label{sec:mosaicity}
Imperfect 2D powders exhibit out-of-plane mosaicity, with crystallite layer-normals (van der Waals crystal normals) distributed about the substrate normal (Fig.~\ref{fig:mosaic}). 

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.45\linewidth]{mosaic/mosaic_schematic.png}
  \caption{Schematic of crystallite layer-normal misorientation (mosaicity). A distribution of crystallite orientations may form in a thin film.}
  \label{fig:mosaic}
\end{figure}
We describe out-of-plane mosaicity by a misorientation density \(\omega(\Delta\theta)\),
where \(\Delta\theta\) is the crystallite tilt angle relative to the substrate normal (\(\Delta\theta=0\) for perfect alignment). Because orientations are periodic, we treat \(\omega\) as a \(2\pi\)-periodic wrapped distribution (see supporting information, Sec.~S1.1). We model \(\omega\) as a unit-normalized sum of a Lorentzian and a Gaussian:
\begin{equation}
\omega(\Delta\theta)=\eta\,\frac{1}{\pi}\frac{\Gamma}{\Delta\theta^{2}+\Gamma^{2}}
+(1-\eta)\,\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma}\exp\!\left(-\frac{\Delta\theta^{2}}{2\sigma^{2}}\right),
\qquad 0\le\eta\le 1,
\end{equation}
with Lorentzian half-width at half maximum \(\Gamma\) and Gaussian standard deviation \(\sigma\).
The inclusion of the Lorentzian component is not immediately intuitive and will be explained in more detail later in the discussion.

For a reflection, we work in the meridional section (\(G_r, G_z\)) of reciprocal space, where \(G_r\) is the projection of the reciprocal lattice vector onto the plane of the substrate, and \(G_z\) is the component along the surface normal. These components are related to the full reciprocal lattice vector \(\bm G = (H, K, L)\), but the relationship depends on the crystal symmetry. For a general crystal, \(G_r\) is defined as the magnitude of the projection of \(\bm G\) onto the plane, and \(G_z\) is the component along the normal direction. 

The analysis is motivated by considering rotations about the substrate surface normal, allowing for a description of mosaicity without tying directly to \(H, K, L\) coordinates, but rather focusing on the resulting distribution in reciprocal space with line density \(I_0 \omega(\Delta\theta)\), where \(I_0\) is the total intensity associated with a given reflection prior to redistribution by mosaicity.

Random in-plane rotations about the film normal revolve the meridional line density
about the \(G_z\) axis, generating an axially symmetric distribution on the Bragg sphere.

We normalize the corresponding Bragg-sphere surface density \(\sigma(\vartheta)\) so that \(\int \sigma(\vartheta)\,dA = I_0\), yielding
\begin{equation}
  \sigma(\vartheta)=\frac{I_0}{2\pi R^2\sin\vartheta}\,\omega(\vartheta),
  \qquad 0<\vartheta<\pi,
  \label{eq:mosaic_sigma_maintext}
\end{equation}

where \(R\) is the Bragg-sphere radius for the reflection. The factor \(1/\sin\vartheta\) is the Jacobian for converting the one-dimensional tilt distribution \(\omega\) into a surface density.

The reciprocal-space consequences appear on an area detector in
Fig.~\ref{fig:mosaic_pbi2}. Strong out-of-plane mosaicity concentrates specular
intensity near discrete $G_z$ values at $G_r=0$ (region A), while random
in-plane orientation distributes in-plane reflections uniformly in azimuth
about $G_z$, producing ring-like features (region B).

In the in-plane region (Fig.~\ref{fig:mosaic_pbi2}B), the Ewald sphere intersects the broadened Bragg-sphere feature along an extended band. Azimuthal averaging then produces band-like scattering whose radial width and line shape are set primarily by the core of $\omega$ and by the Jacobian factor $1/\sin\vartheta$ in Eq.~\eqref{eq:mosaic_sigma_maintext}.
In the specular region (Fig.~\ref{fig:mosaic_pbi2}A), the same mosaic spread produces cap-like broadening about the specular reflections. Off-condition specular intensity arises from crystallites whose layer normals are tilted by $\Delta\theta$ away from the mean, so the decay of intensity away from the nominal condition is governed primarily by the tails of $\omega$ (with only weak variation from the geometric factor in Eq.~\eqref{eq:mosaic_sigma_maintext}).

This observation explains why multiple specular reflections can remain visible for a single incident $\mathbf{k}_i$, even when their nominal $\theta_i$ conditions lie outside the measurement window. If $\omega$ contains a Lorentzian component ($\eta>0$), then for $\lvert\Delta\theta\rvert\gg\Gamma$,
\[
\omega(\Delta\theta)\sim \eta\,\frac{\Gamma}{\pi\,\Delta\theta^2},
\]
so the intensity decays algebraically rather than exponentially. In practice this makes the specular reflections a strong constraint during refinement, since peak persistence and line shape at large $\Delta\theta$ directly determine both the width and the tail character of $\omega$, while in-plane rings are less sensitive to the tail.
