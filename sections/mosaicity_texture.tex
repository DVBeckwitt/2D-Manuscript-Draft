\section{Mosaicity}
\label{sec:mosaicity}

Imperfect 2D powders exhibit out-of-plane mosaicity: crystallite layer normals are distributed about the substrate normal (Fig.~\ref{fig:mosaic}).

\begin{figure}[!ht]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.50\linewidth]{mosaic/mosaic_schematic.png}
  \caption{Schematic of out-of-plane mosaicity: layer-normal misorientation relative to the substrate normal.}
  \label{fig:mosaic}
\end{figure}

We model mosaicity with a misorientation density \(\omega(\Delta\theta)\), where \(\Delta\theta\) is the tilt of a crystallite layer normal away from the substrate normal. We take \(\omega\) to be a unit-normalized mixture of a Lorentzian and a Gaussian:
\begin{equation}
\omega(\Delta\theta)=
\eta\,\frac{1}{\pi}\frac{\Gamma/2}{\Delta\theta^{2}+(\Gamma/2)^{2}}
+(1-\eta)\,\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma}\exp\!\left(-\frac{\Delta\theta^{2}}{2\sigma^{2}}\right),
\qquad 0\le\eta\le 1,
\label{eq:mosaic_kernel}
\end{equation}
where \(\Gamma\) is the Lorentzian full-width at half maximum and \(\sigma\) is the Gaussian standard deviation. We use the \(2\pi\)-periodic wrapped form of \(\omega\) (normalized on \((-\pi,\pi]\)); see SI for details.

For each reflection we work in the meridional \(\{G_r,G_z\}\) section of reciprocal space, with \(G_z=\bm G\cdot\hat{\bm n}\) and \(G_r=\lVert \bm G-(\bm G\cdot\hat{\bm n})\hat{\bm n}\rVert\), where \(\hat{\bm n}\) is the film (substrate) normal. Mosaicity redistributes intensity along the Bragg sphere in polar angle. We write the resulting meridional line density as \(I_0\,\omega(\Delta\theta)\), where \(I_0\) is the integrated intensity weight of the reflection before mosaic broadening (so \(I_0\,\omega\) has units of intensity per radian).

Random in-plane rotations about \(\hat{\bm n}\) revolve this meridional line density about the \(G_z\) axis, generating an axially symmetric distribution on the Bragg sphere. A latitude ring at polar angle \(\theta\) has area element \(dA=2\pi R^{2}\sin\theta\,d\theta\), so intensity conservation
\(I_0\,\omega(\theta-\theta_B)\,d\theta=\sigma(\theta)\,dA\) gives
\begin{equation}
  \sigma(\theta)=\frac{I_0}{2\pi R^2\sin\theta}\,\omega(\theta-\theta_B),
  \qquad 0<\theta<\pi,
  \label{eq:mosaic_sigma}
\end{equation}
where \(\theta_B\) is the Bragg-sphere polar angle for the ideal (perfectly aligned) crystallite.

Figure~\ref{fig:mosaic_combo} shows how mosaic broadening in \(\{G_r,G_z\}\) maps to detector-space arcs. When \(G_r\neq 0\) (in-plane condition), the Ewald sphere intersects the broadened Bragg-sphere feature in two symmetric arcs. When \(G_r=0\) (specular condition), intensity concentrates near the polar cap, producing a single arc.

On the detector, in-plane features appear as band-like scattering whose radial width and line shape are set primarily by the core of \(\omega\) and by the Jacobian factor \(1/\sin\theta\) in Eq.~\eqref{eq:mosaic_sigma}. Specular features are especially sensitive to the tails of \(\omega\), because intensity observed far off the nominal condition is controlled primarily by the tail character. In particular, a Lorentzian component produces algebraic decay at large \(|\Delta\theta|\), increasing the persistence of off-condition specular intensity and strengthening constraints on \(\Gamma\) and \(\eta\) during refinement.
