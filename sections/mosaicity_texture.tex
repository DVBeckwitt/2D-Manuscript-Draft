¥subsection{Mosaicity}
¥label{sec:mosaicity}
The mosaic picture is most easily understood by comparing the three orientational limits introduced in Fig.‾¥ref{fig:intro_single_recip}, Fig.‾¥ref{fig:intro_3d_recip}, and Fig.‾¥ref{fig:intro_2d_recip}. For a perfect single crystal (Fig.‾¥ref{fig:intro_single_recip}), a reflection carries its full weight at a single Bragg spot. For a 3D powder (Fig.‾¥ref{fig:intro_3d_recip}), random crystallite orientations spread that weight uniformly over the Bragg sphere, so the Ewald-sphere intersection gives a circle of equivalent reciprocal-space points and associated exit vectors $¥mathbf{k}_f$. A 2D powder (Fig.‾¥ref{fig:intro_2d_recip}) lies between these limits: the allowed $¥mathbf{k}_f$ values still lie on that circle, but out-of-plane mosaicity makes some portions of the circle more probable than others. Mosaicity is therefore treated as a redistribution of the ideal per-reflection intensity over the allowed ¥(¥mathbf{k}_f¥) family, rather than as a change to the underlying diffraction condition or structure factor.

For a reflection, we work in the meridional section (¥(G_r, G_z¥)) of reciprocal space, where ¥(G_r¥) is the projection of the reciprocal lattice vector onto the plane of the substrate, and ¥(G_z¥) is the component along the surface normal. In this section, the perfect-crystal Bragg spot appears as a point on the Bragg circle. In the full three-dimensional picture, random in-plane rotations generate the circle of equivalent reciprocal-space intersections and associated ¥(¥mathbf{k}_f¥) values described above, consistent with the reciprocal-space evolution from Fig.‾¥ref{fig:intro_single_recip} to Fig.‾¥ref{fig:intro_3d_recip} to Fig.‾¥ref{fig:intro_2d_recip}. Out-of-plane misorientation then replaces the uniform weighting of that ideal circle by a line density ¥(I_0 ¥omega(¥Delta¥theta)¥), where ¥(I_0¥) is the total intensity associated with the reflection before mosaic redistribution.

We describe this out-of-plane mosaicity by a misorientation density ¥(¥omega(¥Delta¥theta)¥), where ¥(¥Delta¥theta¥) is the crystallite tilt angle relative to the substrate normal (¥(¥Delta¥theta=0¥) for perfect alignment). Because ¥(¥Delta¥theta¥) is an angular variable, we treat ¥(¥omega¥) as a ¥(2¥pi¥)-periodic wrapped distribution: ¥(¥Delta¥theta¥) and ¥(¥Delta¥theta+2¥pi¥) are equivalent, and without wrapping the tails would be artificially truncated at the chosen angular boundary (see supporting information, Sec.‾S1.1). We model ¥(¥omega¥) as a unit-normalized sum of a Lorentzian and a Gaussian:
¥begin{equation}
¥omega(¥Delta¥theta)=¥eta¥,¥frac{1}{¥pi}¥frac{¥Gamma}{¥Delta¥theta^{2}+¥Gamma^{2}}
+(1-¥eta)¥,¥frac{1}{¥sqrt{2¥pi}¥sigma}¥exp¥!¥left(-¥frac{¥Delta¥theta^{2}}{2¥sigma^{2}}¥right),
¥qquad 0¥le¥eta¥le 1,
¥end{equation}
with Lorentzian half-width at half maximum ¥(¥Gamma¥) and Gaussian standard deviation ¥(¥sigma¥).
These components are related to the full reciprocal lattice vector ¥(¥bm G = (H, K, L)¥), but the relationship depends on the crystal symmetry. For a general crystal, ¥(G_r¥) is defined as the magnitude of the projection of ¥(¥bm G¥) onto the plane, and ¥(G_z¥) is the component along the normal direction. The inclusion of the Lorentzian component will be motivated later by the observed persistence of specular intensity away from the nominal condition.

Random in-plane rotations about the film normal revolve the meridional line density
about the ¥(G_z¥) axis, generating an axially symmetric distribution on the Bragg sphere.

We normalize the corresponding Bragg-sphere surface density ¥(¥sigma(¥vartheta)¥) so that ¥(¥int ¥sigma(¥vartheta)¥,dA = I_0¥), yielding
¥begin{equation}
  ¥sigma(¥vartheta)=¥frac{I_0}{2¥pi R^2¥sin¥vartheta}¥,¥omega(¥vartheta),
  ¥qquad 0<¥vartheta<¥pi,
  ¥label{eq:mosaic_sigma_maintext}
¥end{equation}

where ¥(R¥) is the Bragg-sphere radius for the reflection. The factor ¥(1/¥sin¥vartheta¥) is the Jacobian for converting the one-dimensional tilt distribution ¥(¥omega¥) into a surface density. This surface density supplies the weights used to distribute the ideal intensity ¥(I_0¥) over the neighboring ¥(¥mathbf{k}_f¥) rays generated by misoriented crystallites.

The reciprocal-space consequences appear on an area detector in
Fig.‾¥ref{fig:mosaic_pbi2}. Strong out-of-plane mosaicity concentrates specular
intensity near discrete $G_z$ values at $G_r=0$ (region A), while random
in-plane orientation distributes in-plane reflections uniformly in azimuth
about $G_z$, producing ring-like features (region B).

In the in-plane region (Fig.‾¥ref{fig:mosaic_pbi2}B), the Ewald sphere intersects the broadened Bragg-sphere feature along an extended band. Azimuthal averaging then produces band-like scattering whose radial width and line shape are set primarily by the core of $¥omega$ and by the Jacobian factor $1/¥sin¥vartheta$ in Eq.‾¥eqref{eq:mosaic_sigma_maintext}.
In the specular region (Fig.‾¥ref{fig:mosaic_pbi2}A), the same mosaic spread produces cap-like broadening about the specular reflections. Off-condition specular intensity arises from crystallites whose layer normals are tilted by $¥Delta¥theta$ away from the mean, so the decay of intensity away from the nominal condition is governed primarily by the tails of $¥omega$ (with only weak variation from the geometric factor in Eq.‾¥eqref{eq:mosaic_sigma_maintext}).

This observation explains why multiple specular reflections can remain visible for a single incident $¥mathbf{k}_i$, even when their nominal $¥theta_i$ conditions lie outside the measurement window. If $¥omega$ contains a Lorentzian component ($¥eta>0$), then for $¥lvert¥Delta¥theta¥rvert¥gg¥Gamma$,
¥[
¥omega(¥Delta¥theta)¥sim ¥eta¥,¥frac{¥Gamma}{¥pi¥,¥Delta¥theta^2},
¥]
so the intensity decays algebraically rather than exponentially. In practice this makes the specular reflections a strong constraint during refinement, since peak persistence and line shape at large $¥Delta¥theta$ directly determine both the width and the tail character of $¥omega$, while in-plane rings are less sensitive to the tail.
