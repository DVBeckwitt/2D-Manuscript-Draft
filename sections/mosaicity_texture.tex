\subsection{Mosaicity}
\label{sec:mosaicity}

Mosaicity is the out-of-plane orientational spread of the layered crystallites about the mean film normal. It is distinct from the random in-plane rotation that defines the two-dimensional powder limit. Random in-plane rotation generates the equivalent $m$-indexed rod family at a common in-plane reciprocal-space radius $Q_R$, while mosaicity determines which tilted members of that family can satisfy the Ewald condition at a fixed incident angle.

The reason mosaicity must be introduced early is visible in the detector data themselves. A single incident angle should select only a limited part of the specular $m=0$ family if the film has only a narrow orientational distribution. The measured images instead contain unexpected $00L$ intensity, including a low-$L$ feature near the $003$ region and weak off-condition specular peaks that remain visible even when the strongest peaks are plotted on a logarithmic scale. Figure~\ref{fig:raw_detector_mosaic_motivation} should show the full measured detector image before any model overlay, and Fig.~\ref{fig:hk0_low_l_star} isolates the local feature that motivates the long-tail component of the mosaic model.

The main point is not simply that the peaks are broad. The in-plane rod-family arcs are largely controlled by the narrow mosaic core and by instrumental resolution. The specular-family features expose a different part of the orientational distribution: weak intensity from crystallites tilted far enough from the mean normal to satisfy reflections that would otherwise be missed at the nominal incidence condition. A rapidly decaying Gaussian-like mosaic density can reproduce the central widths of near-condition features but does not provide enough tilted-crystallite population in the tails. We therefore use a compact two-component angular density,
\begin{equation}
  \omega_{\mathrm w}(\Delta\theta;\eta,\Gamma,\sigma)
  =\eta L_{\mathrm w}(\Delta\theta;\Gamma)
  +(1-\eta)G_{\mathrm w}(\Delta\theta;\sigma),
  \qquad 0\le \eta \le 1 .
  \label{eq:mosaic_two_component_maintext}
\end{equation}
Here $G_{\mathrm w}$ is the Gaussian-like core, $L_{\mathrm w}$ is the Lorentzian-like tail, $\eta$ is the Lorentzian weight, and $\Delta\theta$ is the crystallite tilt relative to the mean layer normal. The two widths are independent: the Lorentzian full width at half maximum is $2\Gamma$, while the Gaussian full width at half maximum is $2\sqrt{2\ln2}\,\sigma$. The wrapped form conserves probability over one angular period and avoids an artificial discontinuity at $\Delta\theta=\pm\pi$.

The Lorentzian form should be read as a useful long-tail approximation rather than as a claim that the microscopic distribution is exactly Lorentzian. What matters experimentally is the separation of roles. The Gaussian-like core controls the dominant widths of the strong, near-condition features, while the long tail accounts for weak specular-family intensity that appears outside the narrow-core expectation. This is why the mosaic model is constrained most directly by the $m=0$ profiles and by the low-$L$ detector feature, not only by the widths of the brighter off-specular arcs.

Figure~\ref{fig:mosaic_combo} summarizes how the same orientational distribution appears in reciprocal space and detector space. For $G_R\neq0$, random in-plane rotation and out-of-plane mosaicity produce off-specular arcs. For $G_R=0$, the same angular distribution produces cap-like $00L$ intensity near the specular direction, where the tail contribution is especially visible.
