\section{Introduction}
Layered van der Waals solids and related two-dimensional materials are commonly characterized by wide-angle x-ray scattering (WAXS) with area detectors, because a single exposure maps a large reciprocal-space volume and directly reports both structure and orientation.\cite{SmilgiesBlasini2007,Smilgies2009,Hexemer2015,Smilgies2025Crystals,Savikhin2020} Orientation is often the primary performance variable in these anisotropic films.\cite{Novoselov2004,GeimGrigorieva2013,Fischer2023MOFGIWAXS} In layered halides such as PbI$_2$, transport and device response can depend strongly on whether the layers lie parallel to the substrate.\cite{Arendse2023} In topological bismuth chalcogenides, strong $c$-axis texture is needed to preserve the desired electronic response.\cite{Jerng2013} In transition-metal dichalcogenides, catalytic activity increases when orientation exposes platelet edges.\cite{Li2011}

A common thin-film texture in these systems is a two-dimensional (2D) oriented powder where plate-like crystallites lie flat with aligned layer normals but remain azimuthally randomized in-plane, as sketched in Fig.~\ref{fig:intro_realspace_2d}.\cite{Kaganer1999,Smilgies2000POPOP,Fischer2023MOFGIWAXS} This texture is neither a single crystal nor a fully isotropic three-dimensional (3D) powder. As a result, analysis strategies that assume discrete reciprocal-lattice points (single crystals) or spherical powder averaging (3D powders) can fail or become ambiguous for layered films in the intermediate regime.\cite{Kaganer1999}

Despite this hybrid information content, many WAXS workflows reduce the area-detector image to 1D profiles
by azimuthal integration, often with masking near the direct beam and specular region.\cite{Ashiotis2015,Hexemer2015,Strzalka2018,Reus2024,Savikhin2020}
These steps are well suited to nearly isotropic powders, but for 2D powders they deweight or remove the arcs
and near-specular caps that are most sensitive to out-of-plane correlations, stacking, and mosaic tails.\cite{SmilgiesBlasini2007,Hexemer2015}
Stacking disorder is also commonly inferred from 1D interference models and refinements,\cite{HendricksTeller1942,Treacy1991,CasasCabanas2016}
which do not directly exploit the full two-dimensional anisotropy of oriented-film scattering.
More general intermediate textures can be treated with ODF-based refinements or preferred-orientation corrections.\cite{March1932,Dollase1986,MatthiesVinel1982,Pawlik1986,VonDreele1997,LutterottiVasinWenk2014}
In WAXS, recovering a flexible ODF typically requires extensive reorientation data and added degrees of freedom.\cite{Sinha1988,Renaud2009}
We therefore assume the physically motivated 2D-powder texture a priori (axial symmetry with a low-dimensional
tilt kernel) and refine its parameters by forward-modeling the full 2D detector image, so that the arc and
near-specular line shapes in Fig.~\ref{fig:intro_2x3} act as direct constraints to reduce computational resources while improving fit precision.\cite{SmilgiesBlasini2007,Hexemer2015}

Figure~\ref{fig:intro_2x3} summarizes the associated reciprocal-space and detector-space limits. In the Ewald construction (top row), elastic scattering requires that reciprocal-space intensity lie on the Ewald sphere of radius $|\mathbf{k}|$.\cite{AlsNielsenMcMorrow2001}  For a single crystal, reciprocal space consists of isolated points (Fig.~\ref{fig:intro_single_recip}), so at fixed incidence angle the Ewald sphere typically misses most reflections and the detector records only sparse spots for special orientations.\cite{AlsNielsenMcMorrow2001} For a 3D powder, random orientations smear each Bragg point into a spherical shell (a Bragg sphere), and the Ewald sphere intersection produces Debye--Scherrer rings on the detector (Fig.~\ref{fig:intro_3d_recip}, Fig.~\ref{fig:intro_3d_image}).\cite{AlsNielsenMcMorrow2001} For a 2D powder, azimuthal disorder about the common \(c\) axis smears each off-specular
Bragg point into an in-plane ring about \(Q_z\), while specular \((00L)\) reflections remain
localized near the \(Q_z\) axis (Fig.~\ref{fig:intro_2d_recip}).\cite{Kaganer1999,SmilgiesBlasini2007}
When the Ewald sphere intersects these features, the detector records hybrid signatures:
pairs of symmetric spots from the ring intersections and single-crystal-like spots near the
specular stripe from \((00L)\) intensity (Fig.~\ref{fig:intro_2d_image}).\cite{SmilgiesBlasini2007,Hexemer2015,Smilgies2005Rods}
Finite mosaicity then redistributes intensity in polar angle, broadening the ring intersections into
arcs and spreading the \((00L)\) spots into cap-like features near the specular region.\cite{Kaganer1999,SmilgiesBlasini2007}



Here we present a quantitative forward model for WAXS from two-dimensional powders that predicts full area-detector images and refines them pixel by pixel against measured data.\cite{Sinha1988,Renaud2009,Breiby2008,Nikolaev2025} The model embeds calibrated detector geometry and grazing-incidence wave propagation within the forward calculation, and it parameterizes texture and disorder explicitly so that reflection profiles in Fig.~\ref{fig:intro_2x3} act as direct constraints.\cite{Sinha1988,Renaud2009,SmilgiesBlasini2007,Savikhin2020} We validate the ordered 2D powder limit using MBE-grown Bi$_2$Se$_3$ and Bi$_2$Te$_3$ films, and we then apply the same framework to disordered PbI$_2$ to extract stacking-fault parameters from crystal truncation rods along $G_z$ that are typically masked in 1D-integrated analyses.\cite{Arendse2023,Jerng2013,Treacy1991,CasasCabanas2016} By embedding geometric and sample effects within a single self-consistent forward model, we reduce correction-induced bias and enable joint refinement of geometry, texture, and disorder directly from the raw images.\cite{Hexemer2015,Reus2024,Nikolaev2025}
