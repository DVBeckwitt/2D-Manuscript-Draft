\section{Discussion and conclusion}

The results show that 2D oriented-powder WAXS patterns can be analyzed quantitatively without reducing the image to either a single-crystal indexing problem or a conventional 3D powder pattern. The forward model keeps detector geometry, sample alignment, mosaic broadening, ordered structure, and stacking disorder within one detector-space calculation, while the refinement separates these terms by the observables that constrain them most directly. Ordered Bi$_2$Te$_3$ and Bi$_2$Se$_3$ provide the baseline case where, once geometry and mosaicity are fixed, the remaining Bragg-intensity variation can be assigned to ordered-structure parameters. PbI$_2$ provides the disorder case. The ordered Bragg model reproduces the peak positions and intensities but leaves structured rod intensity that is reduced by adding the stacking-fault correction.

Peak positions determine geometry before peak widths are interpreted. Local profiles determine the mosaic and instrumental response before integrated intensities are assigned to structure. Selected $Q_r/Q_z$ rod profiles determine stacking disorder only after the ordered baseline is fixed. That order reduces the risk that diffuse scattering is incorrectly absorbed into geometry, mosaicity, or independent peak areas.

The approach is also limited by the information retained in the selected observables. A detector-space forward model cannot remove ambiguity if the chosen reflections do not span enough geometry, mosaic, and intensity constraints. The stacking-disorder parameters are therefore reported as effective descriptors of the selected rod family and fitted model class. Within that scope, the combination of detector-space fitting and selected-rod validation provides a practical route for comparing ordered and faulted layered films using area-detector WAXS data.
